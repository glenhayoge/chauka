# Understanding Results Hierarchies

A results hierarchy (also called a results chain or results framework) is the core structure that organises your logframe. It shows the causal pathway from what you do to the change you want to see.

## The standard levels

Most development organisations use a three-level hierarchy:

### Impact (Goal)
The highest level — the long-term, broad change your programme contributes to. You don't achieve this alone; many actors and factors contribute.

**Characteristics:**
- Long-term (5-10+ years)
- Broad, population-level change
- Your project contributes to but doesn't fully control
- Measured through large-scale surveys or national data

**Example:** "Reduced poverty among smallholder farmers in the Southern Region"

### Outcome (Purpose)
The medium-term change that results from people using your outputs. This is the most important level — it's where real change happens.

**Characteristics:**
- Medium-term (2-5 years)
- Behavioural or systemic change
- Directly influenced by your outputs
- Measured through targeted surveys and assessments

**Example:** "Increased agricultural income among participating farming households"

### Output (Result)
The direct, tangible deliverables of your project. These are fully within your control.

**Characteristics:**
- Short-term (within project period)
- Concrete, countable deliverables
- Directly produced by your activities
- Measured through project records and tracking

**Example:** "500 farmers trained in improved crop management techniques"

## How the levels connect

The levels connect through causal logic:

```
Activities (what you do)
    ↓ produce
Outputs (what you deliver)
    ↓ lead to
Outcomes (what changes)
    ↓ contribute to
Impact (the big picture)
```

Each arrow represents an assumption — something that must be true for the causal link to hold.

## Common terminology variations

Different organisations use different terms for the same concepts:

| Level | Variant 1 | Variant 2 | Variant 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Top | Impact | Goal | Strategic Objective |
| Middle | Outcome | Purpose | Intermediate Result |
| Bottom | Output | Result | Immediate Result |

Chauka lets you customise these labels in your logframe settings to match your organisation's terminology.

## Designing an effective hierarchy

### Keep it focused
- **1 Impact** — your overarching goal
- **2-4 Outcomes** — the key changes you need
- **3-5 Outputs per Outcome** — your main deliverables

### Ensure logical coherence
Ask these questions:
- Are the outputs **sufficient** to achieve the outcome?
- Are the outcomes **necessary** to contribute to the impact?
- Have you identified the **assumptions** between each level?

### Avoid common mistakes

**Outputs that are actually activities:**
- Wrong: "Conduct training workshops" (this is an activity)
- Right: "500 farmers trained in crop management" (this is a deliverable)

**Outcomes that are actually outputs:**
- Wrong: "Training manual produced" (this is a deliverable)
- Right: "Farmers adopt improved crop management practices" (this is a change)

**Impacts that are too narrow:**
- Wrong: "Our project succeeds" (too self-referential)
- Right: "Reduced food insecurity in the target region" (broader change)

## Sub-levels

Some organisations use additional levels:

- **Sub-Outputs** — breaking down outputs into smaller deliverables
- **Intermediate Outcomes** — change that happens between outputs and outcomes
- **Strategic Objectives** — grouping outcomes under broader themes

Chauka supports multiple levels of nesting, so you can structure your hierarchy to match your needs.

## Contribution analysis

The causal links between levels aren't just theoretical — Chauka can quantify them. With **contribution analysis** enabled, you can assign **contribution weights** to output-level results and see how their indicator progress rolls up to outcome-level scores.

For example, if Outcome 1 has three Outputs weighted 50%, 30%, and 20%, Chauka calculates a weighted contribution score showing overall outcome progress based on actual indicator data.

This is useful for:
- Demonstrating which outputs are driving results
- Identifying underperforming areas that need attention
- Providing evidence-based progress reporting to stakeholders

See [Contribution Analysis](/help/features/contribution-analysis) for setup and usage.

## Results hierarchies in practice

A well-designed results hierarchy:
- Tells a clear **story of change**
- Provides a **measurement framework** (each result has indicators)
- Identifies **risks** (through assumptions at each level)
- Guides **resource allocation** (activities and budgets link to outputs)
- Supports **reporting** (progress rolls up from outputs to impact)
- Enables **contribution analysis** (weighted progress from outputs to outcomes)
