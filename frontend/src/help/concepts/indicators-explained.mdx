# Indicators Explained

Indicators are the backbone of your monitoring system. They define exactly what you'll measure to know whether your results are being achieved. This guide explains how to design good indicators and use them effectively.

## What makes a good indicator?

Good indicators are **SMART**:

| Criterion | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Specific** | Clearly defined, no ambiguity | "Number of boreholes producing clean water" not "water access improved" |
| **Measurable** | Can be counted, rated, or quantified | A number, percentage, or ratio |
| **Achievable** | Realistic given your resources and context | Based on baseline data and capacity |
| **Relevant** | Directly related to the result it measures | Measures the change described in the result |
| **Time-bound** | Has a defined period for measurement | "By December 2026" or "per quarter" |

## Types of indicators

### Quantitative indicators
Measured in numbers — counts, percentages, ratios, rates.

- "Number of people trained"
- "Percentage of households with access to clean water"
- "Ratio of teachers to students"

### Qualitative indicators
Measured through judgement, observation, or narrative — but still systematic.

- "Quality of governance in target communities (rated 1-5)"
- "Level of community satisfaction with services (survey score)"

### Process indicators
Measure whether activities are being carried out as planned.

- "Number of training sessions conducted"
- "Percentage of planned field visits completed"

### Outcome indicators
Measure whether change is happening.

- "Percentage increase in crop yield among participants"
- "Reduction in school dropout rate"

## Indicator components

A well-defined indicator has several components:

### Baseline
The starting value before your intervention. You need this to measure change.

- Baseline: 15% of households have clean water access
- Target: 60% of households have clean water access
- Change: 45 percentage points

### Target
What you aim to achieve, set per reporting period.

### Actual
The measured value, collected through your data collection methods.

### Data source
Where the data comes from:
- Project records
- Surveys (household, community, school)
- Government statistics
- Observation
- Beneficiary feedback

### Frequency
How often the indicator is measured:
- Monthly (for activity-level tracking)
- Quarterly (for output indicators)
- Annually (for outcome indicators)
- At baseline, midline, endline (for impact indicators)

## Disaggregation

Disaggregation means breaking down your data by categories to reveal inequalities and patterns. Common disaggregation categories:

- **Gender**: Male, Female, Other
- **Age**: Under 18, 18-35, 36-60, Over 60
- **Location**: Urban, Rural, by district/region
- **Disability**: With disability, Without disability
- **Socioeconomic status**: Income quintiles

In Chauka, you implement disaggregation using **sub-indicators**. Each sub-indicator tracks a category, and the parent indicator shows the aggregate.

### Why disaggregate?

Aggregate numbers can hide important patterns:

> "500 people trained" sounds good — but if 490 are men and 10 are women, there's a gender equity issue.

Disaggregation helps you:
- Ensure equitable reach
- Meet donor requirements (many require gender-disaggregated data)
- Identify underserved groups
- Adjust your approach

## Choosing the right indicators

### At the output level
- Focus on **direct deliverables**
- Should be fully within your control
- Examples: number of items distributed, people trained, facilities built

### At the outcome level
- Focus on **change in behaviour, access, or conditions**
- Influenced by your outputs but also by external factors
- Examples: adoption rates, service utilisation, income changes

### At the impact level
- Focus on **broad, population-level change**
- Your project contributes to but doesn't solely cause
- Examples: poverty rates, mortality rates, literacy rates
- Often measured using national data or large-scale surveys

## Common pitfalls

- **Too many indicators** — focus on 1-3 per result; quality over quantity
- **Measuring outputs as outcomes** — "training delivered" is not the same as "skills improved"
- **Vague indicators** — "community awareness improved" is not measurable
- **Ignoring baseline** — without a starting point, you can't measure change
- **Indicator fatigue** — don't collect data you won't use
