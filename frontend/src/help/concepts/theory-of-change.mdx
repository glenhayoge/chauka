# Theory of Change

A Theory of Change (ToC) explains **how and why** your project will lead to the desired change. While a logframe shows what you'll do and measure, a Theory of Change explains the underlying logic and assumptions.

## What is a Theory of Change?

A Theory of Change is a narrative and visual model that describes:

1. The **long-term change** you want to contribute to
2. The **intermediate changes** (outcomes) needed to get there
3. The **preconditions** for those changes
4. The **causal links** between each step
5. The **assumptions** about why each link should work
6. The **evidence** supporting your causal claims

It answers the question: "Why do we believe that what we're doing will lead to the change we want to see?"

## Theory of Change vs Logframe

These are complementary tools, not alternatives:

| Aspect | Theory of Change | Logframe |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Explains the causal logic | Provides the measurement framework |
| **Format** | Narrative + diagram | Matrix / hierarchy |
| **Focus** | Why change happens | What to measure |
| **Flexibility** | Exploratory, iterative | Structured, defined |
| **Assumptions** | Deeply examined | Listed and monitored |

A good practice is to develop your Theory of Change first, then derive your logframe from it.

## Building a Theory of Change

### Step 1: Start with the long-term goal
What is the ultimate change you want to see? This becomes your Impact in the logframe.

> "Reduced maternal mortality in rural Northern Province"

### Step 2: Map backwards
Work backwards from the goal. What needs to change for this to happen?

> Reduced maternal mortality requires:
> - Better access to emergency obstetric care
> - Improved antenatal care attendance
> - Changed attitudes towards facility-based delivery

### Step 3: Identify your contribution
Which of these changes can your project influence?

> Our project will focus on:
> - Training midwives in emergency obstetric care
> - Strengthening referral systems
> - Community health education

### Step 4: Articulate the causal links
Explain **why** each step leads to the next:

> "Training midwives in emergency obstetric care leads to better outcomes because the main barrier is skill gaps, not equipment or access (evidence: District Health Assessment 2023)"

### Step 5: Make assumptions explicit
What must be true for your logic to hold?

> Assumptions:
> - Trained midwives remain in their posts (not transferred or attrited)
> - Government continues to supply essential medicines
> - Cultural barriers to facility delivery are addressed through community engagement

### Step 6: Identify evidence
What evidence supports your causal claims?

> Evidence:
> - Similar training programmes in neighbouring districts showed 40% improvement in outcomes
> - WHO guidance recommends this approach for rural settings
> - Baseline survey confirms skill gaps as primary barrier

## Theories of Change in Chauka

Chauka's results tree naturally captures the Theory of Change structure:

- **Results hierarchy** = your causal pathway (activities → outputs → outcomes → impact)
- **Assumptions** = the conditions between each level
- **Indicators** = how you'll know whether each step is happening
- **Risk ratings on assumptions** = how confident you are in each causal link

While Chauka doesn't have a separate ToC diagram tool, the Design tab effectively represents your Theory of Change as an interactive, measurable framework.

## Using your Theory of Change

### During design
- Challenge your logic: "Does this really lead to that?"
- Identify gaps: "What's missing in the causal chain?"
- Prioritise: "Where should we focus our resources?"

### During implementation
- Check assumptions: "Are our assumptions still holding?"
- Adapt: "The context has changed — how does our logic need to update?"
- Learn: "What are we learning about what works?"

### During evaluation
- Test the logic: "Did the causal links work as expected?"
- Explain results: "Why did we achieve some outcomes but not others?"
- Improve: "How should we revise our theory for the next phase?"

## Common pitfalls

- **Too linear** — real-world change involves feedback loops and non-linear paths
- **Too static** — theories should evolve as you learn
- **Not evidence-based** — assumptions should be grounded in evidence, not hope
- **Disconnected from logframe** — your logframe should reflect your theory, not the other way around
- **Too complex** — a theory no one can explain is not useful
